01 Introduction

Brain tumors are among the most critical health challenges worldwide, with over 300,000 new cases diagnosed annually. Early and accurate detection is crucial for patient survival.

🧠 MRI Imaging

MRI is the most common imaging technique for detecting abnormal brain tumors, providing detailed soft-tissue contrast without radiation.

⏱️ Manual Limitations

Traditional manual analysis is time-consuming, subjective, and prone to human error — especially with large scan volumes.

🤖 AI Solution

Computer-aided diagnosis using deep learning enables automated, consistent, and fast classification of brain MRI scans.

02 Problem Statement

Manual analysis of brain MRI scans suffers from critical limitations that hinder timely and accurate diagnosis.

VolumeThousands of scans daily in large hospitals create review bottlenecks

04 Proposed Methodology

The system combines Deep Learning classification with OpenCV-based image processing.

🧬 Part 1: Classification (ACU-Net)

  • Attention mechanisms from ImageNet pre-trained ACU-Net
  • Implement Spatial Attention to map boundary regions
  • Implement Channel Attention to extract critical features
  • Data augmentation with vertical flips
  • 253 images: 155 abnormal, 98 normal
  • Achieves up to 100% validation accuracy

🔍 Part 2: Detection (OpenCV)

  • Grayscale conversion for single-channel processing
  • Binary thresholding (threshold = 155)
  • Morphological closing + erosion + dilation
  • Adaptive Canny edge detection
  • Contour extraction & area analysis
  • Red overlay on detected tumor regions

⚡ Enhanced Dual-Pipeline

The system operates in two modes: DL Segmentation Mode (loads trained PyTorch .pth file when available) and Heuristic Mode (OpenCV-based feature analysis with intensity ratio, variance, contour ratio, and bilateral asymmetry scoring). This ensures the app works immediately without GPU or dataset.

📐 ACU-Net Architecture at a Glance

Deep U-Net architecture integrated with Spatial and Channel Attention modules. Combines spatial mapping with feature intensity extraction. Uses a custom Combined Dice and Cross-Entropy Loss function. Input: 224×224 pixels, normalized with ImageNet statistics.
